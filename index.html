Three things to flag to the doctor

None are emergencies, but they're new since November — especially the first.

The one to act on — calmly. Fasting sugar actually improved (91 → 86), so day-to-day glucose looks fine; it's the 3-month average that crept over the line. With a fatty liver this hints at early insulin resistance, and a higher red-cell count (below) can nudge HbA1c up on its own. Very reversible at this stage.

"Thick blood" markers

Hb 15.4→17.6 · HCT 47.2→53.2 · RBC 4.97→5.58
recheck
Low<13
Normal13–17
High17+
Nov · 15.4 Jun · 17.6
Three benign explanations stack up. (1) Heavy smoking through April–mid-May raises red cells and can take 2–3 months after quitting to settle. (2) Living at altitude naturally lifts haemoglobin and haematocrit. (3) The draw looks slightly concentrated (calcium, protein, albumin all up together) — mild dehydration that morning. The lab rechecked the Hb, so it's real; the question is just why. Best next step: repeat ~8–12 weeks fully smoke-free and well-hydrated; if still high, ask about ferritin, EPO and a sleep check.

Iron saturation & inflammation

Iron sat 40→57% · TIBC 323→233 · hs-CRP 0.74→1.06

Iron saturation rose and TIBC dropped — the "plenty of iron on board" pattern, not deficiency; read it with a hydrated repeat plus a ferritin, and don't restart iron supplements until then. hs-CRP ticked to average-risk (1.06) — a small rise that smoking, fatty liver and diet feed, and should fall as those improve.
